GEO vs SEO: What's the Difference?

Published July 2025 · 10 min read

If SEO gets you to the top of Google, GEO gets you cited by ChatGPT. They're not the same thing — and in 2025, you need both.

SEO (Search Engine Optimization) targets traditional search engines like Google and Bing. GEO (Generative Engine Optimization) targets AI-powered search engines like ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews, and Claude.

1. Different Target Engines

SEO optimizes for link-based search engines — Google, Bing, Baidu. These engines crawl pages, index keywords, and rank results by relevance and authority.

GEO optimizes for generative AI engines — ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews, Claude, DeepSeek, Kimi. These engines don't just match keywords; they understand questions and generate answers by synthesizing multiple sources.

When a user asks ChatGPT "what's the best CRM for small business?", the AI doesn't show 10 blue links. It writes a paragraph citing brands it trusts. If your brand isn't in its training data or crawlable sources, you don't exist.

2. Different Crawlers — Different Rules

Traditional SEO cares about Googlebot. You check robots.txt to make sure Google can crawl your site.

GEO cares about a new wave of AI crawlers:

  • GPTBot — OpenAI's crawler (powers ChatGPT)
  • ClaudeBot — Anthropic's crawler (powers Claude)
  • PerplexityBot — Perplexity's crawler
  • Google-Extended — Google's AI training crawler
  • CCBot — Common Crawl (feeds many AI models)
  • Bytespider — ByteDance's crawler (powers Doubao)

Many sites accidentally block these crawlers in robots.txt. That's like blocking Googlebot in 2005. Use our robots.txt AI checker to verify.

3. llms.txt — The New robots.txt for AI

In traditional SEO, robots.txt tells crawlers what they can access. sitemap.xml lists all your pages.

In GEO, a new file called llms.txt serves a different purpose: it tells AI models what your site is about and which pages matter most. Think of it as a curated guide for AI — not a list of URLs, but a structured summary with context.

Learn how to create one in our complete llms.txt guide, or check if your site already has one.

4. Content Strategy: Keywords vs. Citations

SEO content targets specific keywords. You research search volume, optimize title tags, build backlinks. The goal is to rank for a query.

GEO content is different. AI engines don't match keywords — they understand concepts. To be cited, your content needs:

  • ✅ Clear, factual statements — AI extracts sentences as citations
  • ✅ Structured data — JSON-LD schema helps AI parse your content
  • ✅ Statistics and data — AI prefers content with concrete numbers
  • ✅ FAQ format — Question-answer pairs map perfectly to AI queries
  • ✅ Author authority — E-E-A-T signals (Experience, Expertise, Authority, Trust)

The shift is from "what keywords does this page target?" to "what questions does this page answer?"

5. Different Success Metrics

SEO Metrics

  • • Keyword ranking position
  • • Organic click-through rate (CTR)
  • • Domain authority
  • • Number of backlinks
  • • Organic sessions / month

GEO Metrics

  • • Citation frequency in AI answers
  • • Brand mention rate in AI responses
  • • llms.txt presence and quality
  • • AI crawler accessibility score
  • • Structured data coverage

6. They're Complementary, Not Competing

Here's the key insight: GEO doesn't replace SEO. They work together.

SEO gets you ranked on Google → users click through → you get traffic.
GEO gets you cited by AI → users see your brand in answers → they search for you on Google → SEO kicks in.

The most successful sites in 2025 do both: traditional SEO for Google rankings, plus GEO for AI search visibility. Our free GEO audit tool checks 12 dimensions of AI readiness — many of which also benefit your traditional SEO.

7. Practical Steps: Do Both

  1. 1. Keep doing SEO — it's not going away. Google still drives massive traffic.
  2. 2. Check your robots.txt — make sure AI crawlers aren't blocked
  3. 3. Create an llms.txt file — guide AI models to your best content
  4. 4. Add JSON-LD structured data to key pages
  5. 5. Write content in clear, citation-friendly format (facts, stats, FAQs)
  6. 6. Run a GEO audit to find gaps in your AI readiness
  7. 7. Re-audit monthly — AI search is evolving fast
